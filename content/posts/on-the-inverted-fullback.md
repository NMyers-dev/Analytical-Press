The inverted fullback is, at this point, the most over-specified idea in club soccer. It has become a tactical pattern the way the four-chord song became a pattern: ubiquitous, cheap to copy, hard to do well. You can watch two leagues in a weekend and see six of them. Perhaps one will actually be helping.

## What it is, when it works

The short version: in possession, a wide defender steps into midfield, creating a numerical advantage in the center while freeing the near-side winger to stay wide and high. At its best, it pulls an opposing midfielder out of shape and turns a 4-3-3 into something closer to a 3-2-5 without having to change formation on paper.

It works when three conditions are satisfied at once. The fullback can actually pass under pressure. The team has a winger who does not need overlaps to be effective. The opponent is pressing with a midfielder who can be baited into the tuck.

Take away any one of those and you get what we see in the mid-table weekend: a fullback standing in front of his own center backs, passing square, while the winger waits for overlaps that never come.

## The instinct to copy

A lot of this, I think, is downstream of a generation of coaches watching one team solve one problem and taking it away as a principle rather than a solution. It is the same thing that happened when every Premier League team suddenly wanted a sweeper-keeper, regardless of whether the keeper could actually receive a back-pass under real pressure.

Tactical copying is not cowardice. It is how the game advances. But there is a difference between borrowing a mechanism and borrowing a *context*. Most clubs borrow the mechanism.

## A modest suggestion

Before your fullback starts tucking in, ask the following:

1. Can he, *under pressure*, make the diagonal pass that the tuck is supposed to unlock?
2. Does your winger gain anything from being left one-on-one, or does he need an overlapping runner to be effective?
3. When the opponent refuses the bait and stays in a compact mid-block, what is the plan?

If the answer to any of those is a shrug, you do not have an inverted fullback. You have a fullback who has been asked to stand somewhere he does not belong.

There is a quiet dignity in the flat 4-4-2, and no one is writing about it.
