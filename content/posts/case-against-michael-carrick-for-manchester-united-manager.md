When Michael Carrick was first appointed interim manager of Manchester United, there wasn’t much that couldn’t be improved on. The team was in disarray, and uncertainty hung over Old Trafford like a cloud. Kobbie Mainoo seemed desperate to leave in January in search of minutes to hopefully earn a spot at this summer's World Cup, while club captain Bruno Fernandes looked tired and increasingly frustrated with the team’s performance. An Ex-Legend to save a free-falling club is a tale as old as time. Ole Gunnar Solskjaer had already provided safety once, even raising the team from a classic Mourinho 3rd-season catastrophe to a comfortable 2nd-place finish and a run of Champions League qualifications. However, Ole's tenure yielded nothing to show for the “success” found on the pitch. Why will Carrick’s managerial tenure provide anything different?

Short answer: It won’t. Carrick has his team set up remarkably like Ole did. Strong defensive mid-block with explosive counterattacks, accented with incredible displays of talent from wingers. All this with a Bruno Fernandes in the 10 role, a man who excels at receiving the ball on the half turn and spraying out passes as quickly as he can for runners. May I have been a little bit sharp at the start of this paragraph? Does it work? Yes, but there are tons of ways to win a soccer game, of course. The only problem is that faults are showing, faults that took longer to show during Ole’s reign, and I refuse to watch us potentially waste another 3 years with nothing to show.

It is hard to argue against Carrick when his first two wins came against Manchester City and Arsenal, 2-0 and 3-2 wins respectively. Yet actually, those are the teams that the Manchester United teams of Ole and Carrick are built to play against. High lines that allow for plenty of space behind for United’s attacking options to exploit. Ole’s side even did the league double over Pep’s Manchester City, winning spectacularly, specifically at Old Trafford, in the 2019-2020 season. The issue with our tactic isn’t a problem against high-flying, high-possession, high-lined teams. It’s a problem against the teams you need to beat if you plan to win silverware, most importantly, the Premier League.

Those teams are the lower teams, teams that can’t afford the quality to push high, or are smart enough and self-aware enough to know how to frustrate this United Side. We’ve been shown some great examples of this in effect already.

#### **West Ham United 1 - 1 Manchester United**

The first signs of weakness showed in this bad display. West Ham were firmly in a relegation battle and looked there for the taking, but 65% possession alone showed how the game was played. Manchester United held the ball far more and were forced to build up play to break down a stubborn West Ham with strong counter-attacking preferences through their captain Jarred Bowen. We looked toothless and although we had more shots and almost double the amount of passes (551 to 261), it took a 90 + 6’ minute equalizer from the impressively sized Benjamin Sesko to confirm a draw. We were beaten on xG and the eye test, a far too familiar sight to those who remember the crumbling of that Ole team.

#### **Newcastle United 2 - 1 Manchester United**

Down to 10 men right before half time, a stalwart Newcastle side managed to find a winner late to take all three points. The stats tell the same story as before. A dominant possession game for Manchester United, which led to less xG EVEN when excluding the Newcastle penalty. None of that was the worst part; Newcastle was a severely demoralized side. Fresh off Champions League expulsion with nothing to fight for in the league, it was another game where Manchester United should have come away with an easy win. Yet in a simple effort to stop Manchester’s hot run (and being forced to do so with 10 men), a simple low block prevented any real threat to the home team.

#### **Manchester United 1 - 2 Leeds United**

With a little more than 3 weeks off to prepare (barring international players playing for their country), Carrick was given ample time to prepare for the relegation-scrapping Leeds team. United expected to have more possession in this game, but it seemed no one showed up. Again, the same story played out. A possession of 53%, even with a soft Lisandro Martinez red card, with 20 shots and a meager 1.27 xG compared to the 2.57 xG of Leeds. It was feeble, but maybe not in the same way as the other games. This system cannot allow for the seamless transition of players and performances historically seen in title-winning sides such as Pep’s City teams and Klopp’s Liverpool. If one man is down for us, the whole thing seems to fall apart.



I’ll concede that if someone can take us from the despair of Amorim to comfortable clinching Champions League soccer, they should be given a chance, and I honestly wouldn’t be upset about it. I get it, I understand the logic. Carrick hasn’t even been given a window, and if the reports of United’s summer spree are to be believed, he will have a large sum to mold the team to his liking. I just don’t want to see another 200-300 million spent down the drain on players that ONLY can run in behind or sit deep. Manchester United should dominate games and be unwavering in their style, no matter the opponent, performing over 40-50 games rather than the handful we have seen in the past.
