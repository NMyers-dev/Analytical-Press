Expected goals has been the most productive, and the most abused, stat in public soccer analytics. Productive, because it forced a generation of analysts and fans to stop arguing about *shot counts*. Abused, because people keep asking it to do jobs it was never designed for.

A short list of things xG is good at:

- Describing the *quality* of the chances a team created or conceded in a single match.
- Smoothing out single-match noise when aggregated over a season.
- Forcing a conversation about shot location that did not previously exist.

A short list of things xG is bad at:

- Telling you who the best finisher in the league is from ten shots.
- Predicting a single cup tie.
- Summarizing chance quality in games where one team parked the bus and got one clear break.

Most of the critique xG gets is really a critique of people using it badly. That is true of almost every public stat. The right response to someone misusing xG is not to throw out the tool; it is to read the next paragraph.

## What a ten-year-old model actually says

There is a tendency to treat xG as if it were one object. It is not. Every public xG model was trained on its own event data, uses its own shot features, and encodes its own biases. When people argue about "the xG" of a shot they usually mean *someone's* xG, and they rarely ask whose.

That is not an indictment. It is how model-building works. But it is worth saying out loud: when two sources disagree about the xG of a chance, neither is lying. They have different models.

## Where to go from here

The next decade of this work is already happening, mostly quietly, mostly inside clubs. Possession value models are better than xG for most of the questions people wish they could ask xG. Press-adjusted metrics actually describe the sport most teams now play. The question is whether any of that makes it into public work before the discourse moves on.

For our purposes here, we will keep using xG when xG is what the question needs, and put it down when it is not.
